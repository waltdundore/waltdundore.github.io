Building Infrastructure Automation with AI

One person. AI assistance. A mission to help schools.

The Story Behind Ahab

This project started with a simple observation: schools and non-profits are stuck between expensive cloud services, expensive consultants, or complex DIY tools that require expertise they don't have.

I thought: what if there was a fourth option? What if infrastructure automation could be simple, transparent, and built specifically for education?

The Spark

One conversation with Kris Lamoureux sparked this entire journey. His brilliant insight made me realize this was possible. That conversation changed everything.

Thank you, Kris. This wouldn't exist without you.

The Challenge

I'm not a traditional developer. I don't write most of the code myself. I'm not a project manager, though I coordinate complex systems. I'm not a consultant, though I solve infrastructure problems.

I'm doing something that doesn't have a job title yet.

I direct AI to build infrastructure automation while maintaining safety-critical standards, teaching everything along the way, and documenting the entire process so others can learn and continue.

The Method

This project is built using AI assistance (Kiro/Claude). I provide:

  • Requirements and specifications - What needs to exist and why
  • Design decisions - How it should work and what standards to follow
  • Testing and verification - Ensuring it actually works
  • Documentation and teaching - Explaining what was built and why

The AI provides:

  • Implementation speed - Writing code faster than I ever could
  • Consistency - Following patterns and standards rigorously
  • Comprehensive documentation - Explaining every decision

This is a demonstration of what's possible when humans provide clear direction and AI handles implementation.

What I Actually Do (And Why It's Hard to Explain)

"So what do you do?"

I get this question a lot. And I never have a good answer.

Requirements Architect

I figure out what needs to exist and why. Not just "build a web server" but "schools need simple commands that follow security best practices."

AI Manager

I direct AI to implement those requirements. Like a film director - I don't act or run the camera, but I have a vision and coordinate to make it happen.

Quality Controller

I test everything. Every command. Every feature. If it doesn't work in my homelab, it doesn't ship. We eat our own dog food.

Documentation Writer

I explain what was built and why it matters. Not just "how to use it" but "why it works this way" and "what we learned."

Standards Enforcer

I ensure security, safety, and best practices. NASA Power of 10 rules. Zero Trust principles. Schools can't afford data loss.

Systems Thinker

I see how pieces connect and where they'll break. Not just "does this work" but "what happens when this fails at 2am?"

The Reality

This role doesn't have a name yet. I document everything so others can learn from it.

Why This Matters

For Schools

They get working tools built by someone who understands their constraints: limited budget, limited staff, need for transparency.

No expensive consultants. No vendor lock-in. Your data stays yours.

For Students

Learn by doing. Build portfolio projects. Understand how infrastructure works.

For the Industry

This is the future. We're just documenting it as it happens.

For Other Introverts

You don't need to be loud to make an impact. You need to be clear, thorough, and willing to document what you know.

Intelligence without communication dies with you. Documented intelligence lives on.

The Teaching Mission

"We're not here forever, but what we teach can be."

This project isn't just about infrastructure automation. It's about documenting what we know to be true and passing it on.

Every script, every page, every refactor carries a teaching mindset. Not because we're vendors selling a product, but because knowledge dies when it's not shared.

What We Promise

  • Teaching mindset - Every piece of code explains WHY, not just WHAT
  • Radical transparency - Show the failures, not just successes
  • Knowledge transfer - Document so others can learn and carry forward
  • No black boxes - If you can't understand it, we failed

How This Actually Works

1. Human Vision

I identify what schools need. Not just "a web server" but "simple commands that follow security best practices and teach as they work."

↓

2. Detailed Specifications

I write specs that explain not just WHAT to build, but WHY it matters, HOW it should work, and WHAT standards to follow.

↓

3. AI Implementation

AI (Kiro/Claude) writes the code, following NASA Power of 10 rules, Zero Trust principles, and security best practices.

↓

4. Rigorous Testing

I test everything in my homelab. Same commands. Same repository. If it breaks for you, I likely found it first.

↓

5. Comprehensive Documentation

I document what works, what doesn't, what we learned, and why we made each decision. Transparency in action.

↓

6. Open Release

Everything is released openly. Code, specs, lessons learned. Others can learn, use, modify, and improve.

Radical Transparency

We're releasing not just the code, but the specifications that show how it was built. You can see:

  • What requirements I gave the AI
  • What design decisions were made and why
  • What problems we encountered and how we solved them
  • What we learned from failures

This is unprecedented transparency. Most projects hide the messy middle. We document it.
